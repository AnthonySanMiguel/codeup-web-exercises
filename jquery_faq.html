National Park Facts


1. President's Park, Washington DC
As the White House is located on this property, this park consistently houses a U.S. president.

2. Denali National Park, Alaska.
Houses the tallest mountain in North America.

3. Kalaupapa National Historical Park, Hawaii.
Used to be a leper colony.

4. Mammoth Cave National Park, Kentucky.
Consists of 400 miles of caves, chambers, and tunnels available to explore.

5. Death Valley National Park, California and Nevada.
Location for filming the planet Tatooine from the original Star Wars movies.

6. Great Basin National Park, Nevada.
Known for having some of the darkest skies in the continental U.S.

7. Yosemite National Park, California
Contains Giant Sequoias, which are one of the largest living things on the planet.

8. Yosemite National Park, California.
Nearly 1,200 sq. mi., a majority of the park is largely untouched and uninhabited by humans.

9. Canyonlands National Park, Utah.
Location for filming "127 Hours", starring James Franco.

10. Kenai Fjords National Park, Alaska.
Where three-centimeter-long ice worms live inside glaciers, moving through ice using small bristles on their bodies.

Sequoia National Park, California

  • Home to the largest living single-stem tree in the world - the wonderfully named General Sherman. The tree is approximately 275ft (84m) tall and weighs approximately 1,900 metric tonnes.
  • The land's first inhabitants were the Monache Indians.
  • A majority of the park land is only accessible on horseback or on foot, with very little being accessible by roadway.
  • Sequoia trees as found in this park are naturally fire resistant. They have thick bark that can often protect them from burning down.

Florida Everglades

  • Thick sawgrass covers much of the Everglades terrain; and while it may look soft, the razors can cut through your clothing.
  • There are at least nine different ecosystems that work together to sustain life in the Everglades.
  • The the only place in the world where the American alligator and the American Crocodile coexist.
  • Covering over 1.5 million acres, the Everglades is currently at half of its original size.

The National Park of American Samoa

  • The only native mammal species are three species of bats, including large fruit bats with 3-foot wingspans.
  • Over a hundred nearshore species are harvested for food (subsistence fishing is allowed within the park)
  • The only National Park Service site south of the Equator.
  • The park helps protect fa’asamoa—the customs, beliefs, and traditions of the 3,000-year-old Samoan culture.
